Authenticity pass v2: 2026-04-26 — rewritten to the spine Daniel articulated out loud. Share a view; in return, ask about what is in it. One image is a small gesture, but it becomes a data point others draw on. Input side: Classifier reads the photo, Reconciliation verifies it. Output side: Deep Researcher composes everyone's data points into your answer. Beats intact. Filigree removed. Load-bearing phrases preserved: "memory box keyed to the place", "It inherits.", "A fair trade.", "VYU. What's in your view."
Convention: one sentence per line. Paragraph break = beat boundary. All numbers spelled out. Em-dashes preserved as TTS pause markers. Parenthetical "(pause)" markers indicate ~0.8 second silences to splice in post-generation if the model compresses them. Do not feed the parentheticals to the TTS model — strip them or replace with extra punctuation.

----- BEAT 1 — Cold open and problem statement (target 0:00–0:20) -----

In my day job, I help weather satellites see better.
From thirty-six thousand kilometers up, they hover above roughly the same place — and they monitor Europe's weather, all at once.
But as far as they can see, they could not be further from seeing the weather in the eyes of us humans.
Because the weather that matters to you, is the weather in your view.

----- BEAT 2 — The transition (target 0:20–0:55) -----

We carry billions of cameras in our pockets.
What if sharing what you see earned you the right to ask about it?
How is the swell today?
What are the alpine conditions?
Is there still snow on the lone summit?
Weather apps make sense of millions of parameters.
VYU uses those — and adds the view from where you stand, and from everyone standing somewhere else.

----- BEAT 3 — Input side, the photo (target 0:55–1:15) -----

Three Claude Managed Agents do the work — two when you submit, one when you ask.
The Classifier reads your photo.
It names what is in your view, and decides if it belongs on the map.

----- BEAT 4 — Input side, reconciliation (target 1:15–1:38) -----

Reconciliation runs alongside.
It checks your photo against the radar, the satellite, and the official forecast — what agrees, what disagrees.
What comes out is a verified report.
It drops into a memory box keyed to the place — and the map now remembers what you, and others nearby, saw.

----- BEAT 5 — Output side, Deep Researcher (target 1:38–2:00) -----

When you ask, Deep Researcher reads everything — your report, the reports of others nearby, your own history with the map.
If the answer mentions the cool wind off the lake, it is there because someone there felt it.
The agent does not re-derive it.
It inherits.

----- BEAT 6 — The trade (target 2:00–2:25) -----

You took a photo of your sky and asked where to find a beer.
The map named the dark Doppelbock the place is known for — and warned you about the long sleeve, because someone behind the Heiliger Berg had felt the sun drop.
You asked about a Turkish restaurant on holiday.
The map told you about a waterfall eight kilometers away, because someone there had been.
(pause)
One photo.
One question.
A fair trade.

----- BEAT 7 — Validation (target 2:25–2:45) -----

This is the supervised benchmark.
The Classifier hits these numbers on a held-out set — with the thresholds set before the runs, not after.
We are not claiming it is perfect.
We are claiming it is honest.

----- BEAT 8 — Close (target 2:45–3:00) -----

The satellites see the continent.
The people see the moment.
The map composes them.
(pause)
Share a view. Ask a question.
VYU. What's in your view.
